# Difficult placements and adjudications

## How to read this register

This file records placements for which more than one home was genuinely defensible. It does not list every assignment; the complete primary records are in [`philosopher-assignments.csv`](./philosopher-assignments.csv) and [`branch-assignments.csv`](./branch-assignments.csv), and every referenced hall and room is defined in [`hall-program.json`](./hall-program.json).

“Primary” means the location of the complete, countable exhibit. “Secondary” means a focused lens that links back to that primary. Neither term measures a thinker’s importance. Stable IDs are included so the decision can be checked against the machine-readable files.

Specialist review exposed a recurring disagreement between two good instincts:

- **context-first reviewers** wanted a thinker to remain among the texts, institutions, languages, and debates that formed the work;
- **contribution-first reviewers** wanted the thinker near the field in which the work is now most useful to visitors.

The approved program normally gives the primary home to historical formation and uses secondary appearances for later fields. It departs from that rule only where a mature, field-defining contribution gives a substantially clearer and more responsible visitor narrative. Beauvoir’s Feminist Philosophies primary is the principal approved exception; the cases below show both the tradeoff and the locked resolution.

## Major person-level decisions

### Aristotle — ancient primary, many real afterlives

**The disagreement.** A field-first approach could place Aristotle in Logic, Metaphysics, Ethics, Politics, Science, or even Mind. A reception-first approach could place him at the center of Islamic, Jewish, or Latin medieval philosophy. The ancient specialist objected that any of these choices would make a later use of Aristotle stand in for Aristotle’s own fourth-century BCE Academy and Lyceum setting.

**Final assignment.** Aristotle is an `anchor-exhibit` in `wing-mediterranean-antiquity` → `mediterranean-beginnings-classical` → `med-plato-aristotle`.

**Secondary program.** The CSV links him to:

- `core-questions-forum` for logic, reality, mind, science, and other field lenses;
- `moral-life-practical-reason` and `justice-democratic-reason` for ethics and politics;
- `islamic-philosophical-worlds`, `jewish-philosophy`, and `latin-christian-scholastic` for distinct receptions.

These later installations must discuss the receiving thinkers and institutions rather than replaying the ancient biography. Avicenna’s Aristotle, Maimonides’s Aristotle, and Aquinas’s Aristotle are related but not identical curatorial objects.

**Resolution.** The final review accepted the ancient primary once secondary installations were guaranteed at more than token scale. This is the model for a thinker important to many fields: retain one historically truthful center and let each later hall explain its own transformation. [SEP’s Aristotle overview](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/) supports both the breadth of the corpus and its unusually long reception.

### Kant — a late-Enlightenment hinge, not a member of every consequence

**The disagreement.** Kant could plausibly anchor Epistemology, Deontology, Aesthetics, German Idealism, or an Enlightenment hall. The German-Idealism reviewer stressed that Fichte, Schelling, and Hegel are unintelligible without him. The early-modern reviewer countered that the critical project responds directly to the rationalist–empiricist crisis and belongs to the late Enlightenment. The accuracy metadata also marks Kant as a `precursor` to German Idealism, not simply one more German Idealist.

**Final assignment.** Kant is an `anchor-exhibit` in `wing-early-modern-enlightenment` → `enlightenment-revolution-kant` → `enlightenment-kant-critical`.

**Secondary program.** `german-idealism-afterlives` presents the post-Kantian departure point. `core-questions-forum`, `moral-life-practical-reason`, and `justice-democratic-reason` carry knowledge, aesthetics, religion, duty, autonomy, and political implications. The Forum can use multiple room-level lenses without multiplying the primary record.

**Resolution.** Late-Enlightenment primary won because it explains the problem Kant was answering; German Idealism is the successor route explaining what others did with the critical turn. The room is deliberately a threshold, so the building still makes the hinge visible. [SEP’s Kant entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kant/) likewise places the critical project at the end of an Enlightenment crisis while documenting its reach across metaphysics, knowledge, ethics, and aesthetics.

### Wittgenstein — one longitudinal analytic exhibit

**The disagreement.** The early work invites a Logic placement; the later work invites Philosophy of Language; parts of the later corpus invite Mind, perception, intention, ethics, religion, or aesthetics. Splitting him into “early” and “late” primaries would make these differences easy to display, but would harden one contested periodization and violate the one-primary rule.

**Final assignment.** Wittgenstein is an `anchor-exhibit` in `wing-modern-traditions` → `analytic-traditions` → `analytic-wittgenstein`.

**Secondary program.** The `core-questions-forum` supplies Logic & Language and Mind & Self lenses, while `moral-life-practical-reason` can address ethical method and forms of life without claiming that ethics was a separate Wittgensteinian school.

**Resolution.** The analytic reviewer’s longitudinal solution prevailed: one installation stages logical form, the transition in method, use, and philosophical therapy while acknowledging scholarly arguments for both rupture and continuity. A single Language primary was rejected because it would understate logic and the conception of philosophy; a Mind primary was too narrow. [SEP’s Wittgenstein entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/wittgenstein/) explicitly treats his central but contested place in analytic philosophy and the debated relation among phases of his work.

### Simone de Beauvoir — feminist primary, existential and phenomenological secondary

**The disagreement.** The continental specialist favored Phenomenology/Existence because Beauvoir’s ethics of ambiguity, account of situation, and philosophical method developed inside postwar French existential and phenomenological debates. The feminist and social-thought specialist argued that making Feminist Philosophies secondary risks repeating a canon in which *The Second Sex* is treated as an application of somebody else’s system, and that Beauvoir’s enduring feminist contribution is at least as defining.

**Final assignment.** Beauvoir is an `anchor-exhibit` in `wing-ethics-politics-society` → `feminist-philosophies` → `feminist-situated-freedom`.

**Secondary program.** `phenomenology-existence-embodiment` receives an **anchor-strength secondary**, not a small route label. It concentrates on ambiguity, situation, existential ethics, phenomenological method, embodiment, and Beauvoir’s place in postwar French debate. It links to the full Feminist Philosophies primary and must present Beauvoir as an original philosopher rather than as Sartre’s follower.

**Resolution.** The approved decision gives priority to Beauvoir’s independent reorganization of feminist philosophy. Making Feminist Philosophies primary prevents *The Second Sex* from being framed as a thematic application of somebody else’s system, while the anchor-strength PEE secondary preserves the existential and phenomenological formation that made her account of situated freedom possible. Three safeguards are locked with the decision: Beauvoir is never presented as Sartre’s derivative; existential ethics and phenomenological method remain integral to the full interpretation; and the PEE secondary must be substantial. [SEP’s Beauvoir entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/beauvoir/) documents her original contributions across phenomenology, existentialism, ethics, feminist philosophy, and political thought.

### Elizabeth Anscombe — action and intention, with ethics as a major secondary

**The disagreement.** The analytic specialist treated *Intention*, ordinary-language method, and action explanation as the thread unifying Anscombe’s work. The ethics specialist emphasized “Modern Moral Philosophy,” her attack on consequentialism, and her role in the modern virtue-ethics revival.

**Final assignment.** Anscombe is a `standard-individual-exhibit` in `wing-modern-traditions` → `analytic-traditions` → `analytic-action-intention`.

**Secondary program.** `moral-life-practical-reason` receives a substantial virtue, moral psychology, and anti-consequentialism lens; `core-questions-forum` connects action to Mind & Self.

**Resolution.** The final review favored Analytic/Action because action and intention provide a through-line rather than one episode of reception. The ethics case was not rejected: the secondary must show how her action theory and moral philosophy constrain one another. [SEP’s Anscombe entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/anscombe/) treats both action theory and moral philosophy as continuing centers of her influence.

### Albert Camus — beside existentialism, never silently enrolled in it

**The disagreement.** Camus is routinely taught with existentialism, but he separated himself from the label. “Absurdism” would be a clearer positive name, but the Atlas has no `absurdism` branch ID. Political revolt is central to *The Rebel* and his journalism, yet a Political Thought primary would understate absurdity, mortality, and the question of how to live.

**Final assignment.** Camus is a `standard-individual-exhibit` in `wing-modern-traditions` → `phenomenology-existence-embodiment` → `existentialism-situated-absurd`.

**Secondary program.** `justice-democratic-reason` carries revolt, violence, political judgment, and his conflicts with revolutionary politics. `colonialism-race-liberation` stages his French-Algerian settler position, refusal to endorse Algerian independence, colonial exclusions, and a critical Fanon counterpoint; this is neither membership nor endorsement.

**Resolution.** The shared room won only because its title names “the absurd and revolt” and the record carries `self-rejected-label`. Labels must say “in conversation with existentialism,” not “Camus the existentialist.” The program does not invent a branch to solve a room-planning problem. His settler-colonial contradiction and Algerian/French settings must remain visible rather than reducing him to a Parisian school. [SEP’s Camus entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/camus/) explains both his forceful separation from existentialism and the existential character of the questions he pursued.

### Michel Foucault — genealogy and subject formation as the center

**The disagreement.** Political and social-thought reviewers could reasonably place Foucault with institutions, prisons, sexuality, and power. Feminist and coloniality routes are also central to his reception. The continental reviewer argued that archaeology, genealogy, historical method, knowledge, and subject formation provide the more coherent account of his own project.

**Final assignment.** Foucault is an `anchor-exhibit` in `wing-modern-traditions` → `critique-power-deconstruction` → `critique-genealogy-power`.

**Secondary program.** The CSV links `justice-democratic-reason`, `feminist-philosophies`, and `colonialism-race-liberation`.

**Resolution.** Genealogy/Power won because it can hold method, institutions, knowledge, and subject formation together. The secondary halls must distinguish later uses of Foucault from his own positions; in particular, he is not enrolled retrospectively as a feminist or decolonial-school member. Nor should the broad `continental-philosophy` label imply one coherent Continental doctrine. [SEP’s Foucault entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/foucault/) supports archaeology, genealogy, institutions, sexuality, and the formation of modern subjects as connected phases of the work.

## Cultural-historical and cross-field decisions

### Buddhist epistemology — pramāṇa in a Buddhist home, with Knowledge and Logic routes

**The disagreement.** An epistemology reviewer favored the Core Questions Forum because Dignāga and Dharmakīrti are indispensable to perception, inference, proof, and philosophy of language. The Buddhist and South Asian reviewers warned that moving them into a modern general field would detach pramāṇa argument from Buddhist metaphysics, soteriology, monastic debate, and its exchanges with non-Buddhist Indian schools.

**Final assignment.** The `buddhist-epistemology` branch is a `thematic-cluster-participant` in `wing-buddhist-worlds` → `buddhist-philosophies` → `buddhist-pramana`. Dignāga is an `anchor-exhibit` and Dharmakīrti an individually searchable `standard-individual-exhibit` in the same room. All three link secondarily to `core-questions-forum`.

**Resolution.** Cultural-historical primary won. “Buddhist epistemology” is explained as a convenient modern umbrella for a pramāṇa lineage, not as a timeless school name or a merely religious version of Western epistemology. The dedicated room prevents the cultural placement from becoming generic “Buddhism”; the Forum secondary prevents its logical and epistemological importance from becoming invisible. [SEP’s Classical Indian Epistemology](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epistemology-india/) situates logic inside long inter-school pramāṇa debates, and [SEP’s Dharmakīrti entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/dharmakiirti/) gives the specifically Buddhist epistemological and linguistic context.

### Islamic, Jewish, and Latin worlds — connected, not collapsed

**The disagreement.** A predominantly chronological museum could put all current medieval records in one hall. A purely thematic museum could distribute them among Metaphysics, Religion, Logic, and Politics. Both would save space. The medieval specialists judged both options reductive: “Islamic philosophy” would become a translation bridge to Europe, Jewish philosophy would disappear inside a Christian period label, and “scholasticism” would become a synonym for an entire millennium.

**Final program.** `wing-medieval-connected-worlds` contains three distinct halls:

- `islamic-philosophical-worlds`, with translation-era falsafa, Avicennian system, kalām and critique, al-Andalus, and post-Avicennian continuities;
- `jewish-philosophy`, with Jewish kalām, poetry, law, revelation, and a Maimonidean crossroads;
- `latin-christian-scholastic`, with transmission, early dialectic, high-university contests, later alternatives, mysticism, and political conflict.

The `islamic-philosophy` branch is an anchor in the first hall. The `medieval-scholasticism` branch is an anchor in the Latin hall and is described as a practice and institutional culture, not all Latin Christian thought. Maimonides is primary in the Jewish hall, with Islamic and Latin secondaries. There is no current Jewish-philosophy branch record, so the program creates the necessary hall but does not fabricate an ID or a forty-fourth branch assignment.

**Resolution.** Cultural distinctness and visitor connection are both preserved by separate halls with reciprocal threshold exhibits. Arabic is not treated as synonymous with Muslim: Christian translators and Jewish philosophers writing in Arabic-speaking settings are named. Greek-to-Arabic and Arabic-to-Hebrew/Latin translation are shown as selection, commentary, reconstruction, and new argument—not passive storage. Useful baselines are [SEP on Greek sources in Arabic and Islamic philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/arabic-islamic-greek/), [SEP on Arabic and Islamic influence in the Latin West](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/arabic-islamic-influence/), [SEP on Maimonides](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/maimonides/), and [SEP on medieval philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/medieval-philosophy/).

### Legalism — a retrospective name for diverse fa currents

**The disagreement.** The source inventory uses the stable branch ID `legalism`, and Han Feizi is a major systematizer, so a normal school display would be easy. The Chinese-philosophy reviewer objected that “Legalism” suggests a unified school and narrows *fa* to law, both historically misleading. A Political Philosophy primary would correct none of that and would extract the debate from Warring States China.

**Final assignment.** The `legalism` branch is a `thematic-cluster-participant` in `wing-east-asian-worlds` → `classical-chinese-traditions` → `china-mohist-fa`. Han Feizi is a `standard-individual-exhibit` there. Both receive political-thought routes through `justice-democratic-reason` as appropriate.

**Resolution.** The stable Atlas name remains searchable, but visitor-facing interpretation leads with “fa and statecraft currents” and explains that *fa* includes standards, models, methods, and administrative norms as well as law. The shared room with Mohism is a capacity choice, not a claim that Mohists and fa thinkers formed one school. It requires separate bays and an explicit debate line; if either roster grows, this is one of the first rooms to split. [SEP’s Legalism entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/chinese-legalism/) calls “Legalism” an inaccurate designation and explains the broader fa tradition.

### Patañjali — Yoga’s own setting before modern Mind

**The disagreement.** Patañjali is highly relevant to attention, cognition, consciousness, practice, and philosophy of mind. The Indian specialist warned that a Mind primary would recast liberation, discipline, metaphysics, and a living Yoga tradition as early material for a modern Western field.

**Final assignment.** Patañjali is a `thematic-cluster-participant` in `wing-south-asian-worlds` → `classical-south-asian-worlds` → `south-yoga-mind-liberation`, with `core-questions-forum` as a secondary association.

**Resolution.** South Asian/Yoga context won. The cluster tier reflects attribution and chronology caution, not lesser importance. The displayed range is a floruit/authorship uncertainty, never a four-hundred-year lifespan. The room treats Yoga as philosophical, practical, and liberative; the Mind & Self secondary then compares accounts of attention and consciousness without claiming ownership. [IEP’s Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali entry](https://iep.utm.edu/yoga/) supports the system’s authoritative, philosophical, meditative, and liberative dimensions.

### Mulla Sadra — post-Avicennian continuity beyond a European era boundary

**The disagreement.** Mulla Sadra lived in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, so a rigid global chronology would place him near Descartes, Spinoza, and early-modern Rationalism. Leaving him in a wing called “medieval” risks a false date; moving him to a European early-modern hall risks a worse cultural and intellectual distortion. Omitting him would repeat the outdated story that Islamic philosophy ended with the classical period.

**Final assignment.** Mulla Sadra is a `standard-individual-exhibit` in `wing-medieval-connected-worlds` → `islamic-philosophical-worlds` → `islamic-post-avicennian`, with secondaries in `core-questions-forum` and `rationalism-mind-nature-system`.

**Resolution.** Intellectual continuity overrides the global era bin. The primary follows post-Avicennian, Illuminationist, Shiʿi, mystical, and Safavid debates; the Rationalism secondary is a bounded seventeenth-century comparison, not a claim that Mulla Sadra was a European rationalist. The renamed wing now signals “medieval and post-classical” continuity, but the gateway must still explain the mismatch between global era labels and this intellectual sequence. If the Islamic roster expands, a separate Post-Avicennian and Early Modern Islamic hall is the preferred expansion—not relocation into the European wing. [SEP’s Mulla Sadra entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mulla-sadra/) documents this synthesis and its later Islamic legacy; [SEP on Arabic and Islamic philosophy of religion](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/arabic-islamic-religion/) explicitly extends its historical survey to this seventeenth-century continuation.

### Jiddu Krishnamurti — South Asian formation without inherited-school membership

**The disagreement.** Krishnamurti was born in South India, formed inside the international Theosophical movement at Adyar, and often compared with Yoga, Buddhist, or Vedānta discussions of mind and liberation. A South Asian primary would preserve biography but could falsely turn comparison into membership. A Philosophy of Religion primary could overstate affiliation to the organized authorities and paths he rejected. A modern Mind primary risks detaching him from colonial and Theosophical history unless the secondary route is unusually explicit.

**Final assignment.** Krishnamurti is a `standard-individual-exhibit` in `wing-core-questions` → `core-questions-forum` → `core-mind-self`. He has a strong secondary route to `classical-south-asian-worlds` and a room-level comparison with the Forum’s Philosophy of Religion anchor.

**Resolution.** The mature contribution to conditioning, thought, psychological time, fear, attention, relationship, and the observer–observed problem makes Mind & Self the clearest primary visitor narrative. Labels identify him as an independent spiritual philosopher and educator, not a member of Yoga, Vedānta, Buddhism, Jainism, or another inherited school. The South Asian route carries Madanapalle, Adyar, colonial history, Theosophical formation, and cross-cultural comparisons. The Philosophy of Religion comparison concerns his criticism of guru authority, organized allegiance, ritual dependence, and fixed paths; it is criticism and comparison, not affiliation. The [1929 dissolution speech](https://kfoundation.org/dissolution-speech/) is the primary institutional break, while Christine V. Williams’s independent biography supplies caution about chronology and later retrospective claims.

## Living and recently deceased records

Recent and living figures raise a different classification risk: a current reputation can look like a final verdict. Nagel, Nussbaum, Butler, and Davis therefore receive normal, searchable primary homes but not “culmination” language. Recently deceased figures, including Jürgen Habermas, remain subject to the same rule. An anchor tier here means that the record organizes a strong visitor question now; it does not predict permanent canonical rank.

The interpretive text and factual life-date fields should be reviewed when the source record changes and on a regular editorial cycle. The modern wings also retain reserve capacity so new work can be added without treating every addition as the endpoint of a linear story.

## Remaining judgment calls and expansion triggers

The masterplan resolves enough to build a coherent program, but it should not pretend every judgment is permanent.

1. **Beauvoir’s primary home is locked in Feminist Philosophies.** If the PEE secondary cannot carry anchor-scale interpretation, expand or redesign that secondary rather than silently reverting the approved primary assignment.
2. **The Mohist/fa shared room** is acceptable only with distinct bays and explicit disagreement. Split it when either branch gains several records or when a visitor test shows conflation.
3. **The Connected Medieval & Post-Classical wing title** now makes Mulla Sadra’s continuation visible, but a larger post-Avicennian roster should still trigger a new hall within the same intellectual-world sequence.
4. **Jewish Philosophy currently lacks a branch record.** Keep the hall and current philosopher homes; add a branch only through a future Atlas content decision, never as a planning convenience.
5. **Buddhist transmission beyond India and Chinese traditions beyond China** have reserved continuity zones but sparse current records. Expansion should deepen those worlds rather than move current figures into generic thematic halls.
6. **Living-thinker tiers** are provisional and should be re-reviewed rather than inherited automatically by future versions of the Atlas.

These are controlled review points. None changes the present accounting: every one of the 43 branch IDs and 142 philosopher IDs still has exactly one primary physical home.
