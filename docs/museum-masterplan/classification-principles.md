# Classification principles

## Purpose and scope

These rules govern the approved program in [`hall-program.json`](./hall-program.json) and the primary assignments in [`branch-assignments.csv`](./branch-assignments.csv) and [`philosopher-assignments.csv`](./philosopher-assignments.csv). They apply to all 43 current branch records and all 146 current philosopher records.

The basic rule is simple: **a primary physical home is a navigation decision, not a claim that one hall owns the person or idea**. Every record receives exactly one primary wing, hall, and room so visitors can find it and the building can be planned. Secondary appearances then make real intellectual overlap visible without cloning the primary exhibit.

The approved program uses ten wings, twenty-six halls, and 105 rooms or zones. The larger structure is intentionally hybrid:

- historically situated schools, traditions, and most philosophers receive cultural-historical homes;
- broad questions and modern academic fields receive thematic homes;
- carefully identified secondary appearances connect the two.

This avoids both of the main classification failures: forcing every tradition into modern disciplinary categories, and forcing every question into a single chronological corridor.

## Decision order

When plausible homes compete, reviewers apply the following tests in order:

1. **Historical and cultural formation.** Where did the thinker, text, school, or practice actually take shape, and among which interlocutors?
2. **Documented intellectual identity.** What tradition, institution, textual lineage, or method did the record participate in? Self-description matters, but later scholarly categories may still be necessary when clearly labeled.
3. **Defining contribution.** Which room best explains why the record matters without reducing it to one later use?
4. **Continuity of argument.** Which placement preserves the debates the record entered, revised, transmitted, or opposed?
5. **Visitor legibility.** If the first four tests remain tied, choose the home that makes the surrounding story easiest to understand.
6. **Capacity.** Room size is a final tie-breaker only. It must never override a defensible historical classification.

The result is not a mechanical “birthplace rule.” A philosopher may receive a thematic primary home when a field-defining contribution provides the clearest account of the mature work. Whitehead is therefore primary in Reality & Being, Thomas Nagel and Jiddu Krishnamurti in Mind & Self, and Carnap, Popper, and Kuhn in Science. Krishnamurti’s placement is explicitly comparative: his South Asian route preserves biography and intellectual context, while his rejection of inherited spiritual allegiance prevents classification as Yoga, Vedānta, Buddhist, or another school member. Beauvoir’s approved Feminist Philosophies primary is another explicit contribution-first adjudication: it treats her feminist philosophy as an independent reorganization of freedom, embodiment, oppression, and becoming while retaining her existential and phenomenological formation as an anchor-strength secondary. These are bounded exceptions, not a general license to detach non-European thinkers from their traditions.

## Historical placement and thematic placement

### Default historical placement

Give historical-cultural priority to:

- named schools and traditions;
- philosophers whose work is intelligible through a particular school, debate, institution, or textual community;
- commentators, systematizers, reformers, and critics whose role depends on an inherited canon;
- traditions whose questions are inseparable from practices of cultivation, liberation, law, ritual, revelation, commentary, or translation.

This is why Aristotle remains in Classical Athens, Patañjali in Classical South Asia, Dignāga and Dharmakīrti in Buddhist Philosophies, Maimonides in Jewish Philosophy, and Mulla Sadra in the post-Avicennian Islamic room. Their importance to logic, mind, metaphysics, ethics, or religion is represented through secondary lenses.

### Default thematic placement

Give thematic priority to:

- transhistorical question records such as metaphysics, epistemology, mind, logic, language, science, aesthetics, and philosophy of religion;
- modern academic fields whose current branch record is broader than any one founding culture;
- philosophers whose signature contribution is best understood as reorganizing one of those questions and whose historical movement is not a more informative primary home.

The Core Questions Forum is an orientation device, not a claim that its headings are timeless or culturally neutral. Each room must show that the modern field name gathers earlier and non-Western questions that were framed in different vocabularies. For example, the Mind & Self room must not imply that inquiry into mind began with the current branch record’s modern start date; its routes lead back to Yoga, Buddhist analyses of cognition, Greek accounts of soul, early-modern theories, phenomenology, and analytic philosophy.

### Era labels are routes, not sealed boxes

“Ancient,” “medieval,” “early modern,” “nineteenth-century,” “modern,” and “contemporary” are wayfinding scales. They do not impose one universal periodization on every region.

- A long-lived tradition stays with its own historical continuity when a global era boundary would sever its argument.
- A threshold figure may sit at the receiving side of a transition if that is where the record’s curatorial function is clearest. Boethius, for example, is a late-antique intermediary in the Latin transmission room, not an uncomplicated “medieval scholastic.”
- Post-classical developments must not disappear because a wing title begins with “medieval.” Mulla Sadra’s seventeenth-century Safavid context is stated prominently.
- “Contemporary” is open-ended. The building has no terminal room suggesting that philosophy has concluded.

## Umbrella traditions, fields, and specific schools

Not all branch records name the same kind of thing. The presentation tier and label must reveal the difference.

| Record kind | Default treatment | Current examples |
|---|---|---|
| Geographic or civilizational umbrella | Supporting orientation that introduces plurality; never a single doctrine | `ancient-greek`, `indian-philosophy`, `chinese-philosophy` |
| Large, internally differentiated tradition | Anchor orientation followed by distinct schools, periods, and disputes | `buddhist-philosophy`, `islamic-philosophy`, `feminist-philosophy` |
| Specific school or lineage | Anchor or standard exhibit in its historical setting | Stoicism, Jainism, Vedānta, Pragmatism, German Idealism |
| Modern disciplinary field | Thematic anchor with historical lenses | Metaphysics, Epistemology, Logic, Philosophy of Mind |
| Retrospective family label | Supporting orientation that names the label’s limits | `continental-philosophy` |
| Retrospective or modern umbrella for a narrower current | Cluster treatment with an explicit terminology note | `legalism`, `buddhist-epistemology` |
| Practice or institutional culture | Explain the practice rather than treating it as every view in an era | `medieval-scholasticism` |

An umbrella record does not absorb its subtraditions. “Indian Philosophy” is the orientation for a South Asian intellectual world, not a school above Jain, Yoga, Vaiśeṣika, and Vedānta. “Chinese Philosophy” introduces competing and interacting ways, not a doctrine shared by Confucians, Mohists, Daoists, and fa thinkers. “Buddhist Philosophy” remains an internally diverse, transregional tradition rather than a single system.

The building program may contain a hall even when the current Atlas does not contain a matching branch ID. The Jewish Philosophy hall is the clearest example: it provides primary homes for current Jewish philosopher records, but the assignment files do **not** invent a `jewish-philosophy` branch. A later content expansion may add such a record through the normal data-review process.

## People, roles, and memberships

Three independent questions must stay independent:

1. **Where is the primary exhibit?** A physical navigation choice.
2. **What is the person’s relationship to a branch?** A historical claim with a status and confidence.
3. **How large is the presentation?** A visitor-program decision.

An anchor exhibit is not automatically a founder, and a critic or commentator can warrant an anchor. Conversely, a founder label must not be awarded merely because a person is famous.

Use the Atlas membership vocabulary literally:

| Membership status | Curatorial meaning |
|---|---|
| `founder` | Strong evidence supports a foundational role in the identified lineage. Avoid “sole inventor” language unless the evidence warrants it. |
| `central`, `canonical`, `major` | The figure is important within or to the field, without a claim of founding it. |
| `school-systematizer` | The figure organizes, formalizes, or decisively develops an existing current. |
| `commentator` | The contribution works through authoritative texts or inherited interpretation; commentary is intellectual production, not a lesser role. |
| `critic` | The figure is best understood through sustained opposition or revision, not membership. |
| `precursor`, `influence`, `later-reception` | A chronological or conceptual relation without retrospective enrollment in the later school. |
| `associated`, `disputed` | Scholarship or evidence does not justify a firmer claim. State what is disputed. |
| `self-rejected-label` | The later category is useful for comparison, but the person rejected it; the exhibit must say so visibly. |

Examples set the standard:

- Confucius is a foundational source for Confucian tradition, not the founder of “Chinese philosophy.”
- Laozi is treated as an attributed or legendary textual persona, not a securely documented sole author-founder.
- Xunzi is a Confucian systematizer and an influence on later fa thinkers, not therefore a member of “Legalism.”
- Śaṅkara, Rāmānuja, and Madhva are major Vedānta commentators and systematizers, not founders of Vedānta as a whole.
- Dignāga is foundational for the Buddhist pramāṇa lineage; Dharmakīrti is its major systematizer.
- Augustine and Pseudo-Dionysius transform Platonist materials in Christian settings; `later-reception` does not make them members of a pagan Neoplatonic school.
- Kant is a precursor to German Idealism; Kierkegaard is a precursor to Existentialism; Nietzsche is an influence on it; Camus carries the `self-rejected-label` status. These are not interchangeable memberships.

## Presentation tiers

Tiers communicate how a record appears physically; they do not rank intellectual worth.

| Tier | Intended use |
|---|---|
| `anchor-exhibit` | A hall-defining installation with enough space to establish a major debate, lineage, or turning point. |
| `standard-individual-exhibit` | A complete, findable individual treatment with biography, works, contribution, and connections. |
| `supporting-exhibit` | Context necessary to understand the room, often an umbrella, transmission, institution, or retrospective label. |
| `thematic-cluster-participant` | One clearly identified part of a multi-voice or attribution-sensitive presentation. It must remain individually searchable. |
| `gallery-archive-or-study-wall-record` | A concise but substantive record with a primary home; never a silent omission or name-only roll call. |

Tier decisions may change as content deepens without changing classification. Any downscaling must preserve a readable rationale, stable ID, and route to the full Atlas article.

## Primary and secondary appearances

Every branch and philosopher has exactly one primary assignment. A secondary association is allowed only when it teaches a relationship that a visitor would otherwise miss.

A secondary appearance may be:

- a **problem lens**, showing how the record changes a question in another hall;
- a **reception lens**, showing how later readers adopted, transformed, or opposed it;
- a **transmission threshold**, explaining movement among languages, institutions, and communities;
- a **comparison station**, placing two approaches in a carefully bounded dialogue;
- a **route marker**, directing the visitor to the primary exhibit.

A secondary appearance must:

1. display the primary hall title and stable record ID;
2. explain the specific relation to the secondary room;
3. avoid duplicating the complete biography, work list, and media package;
4. avoid turning influence, criticism, or reception into school membership;
5. remain subordinate in assignment counts, search results, and capacity accounting to the one primary record.

The assignment CSVs list hall-level route candidates only. Before implementation, each candidate must be promoted into a typed secondary-association record with a room-level destination, relation type, and one-sentence rationale. Until then, a semicolon-delimited hall ID means “review this route,” never “assert influence.” This matters especially for synchronized comparisons, contested receptions, and cross-cultural transmission.

An “anchor-strength secondary” is an allowed exception in scale, not in identity. Beauvoir’s Phenomenology, Existence, and Embodiment installation is substantial enough to anchor an account of ambiguity, situation, existential ethics, phenomenological method, and postwar debate while pointing to her complete primary exhibit in Feminist Philosophies. It must not present her as an application of Sartre. Wollstonecraft receives a historically specific feminist secondary while her primary biography remains in the Enlightenment hall.

## Cross-cultural transmission and connected worlds

Transmission is not a one-way arrow labelled “influence.” Each transmission exhibit should identify, where the evidence permits:

- the source and receiving communities;
- translators, commentators, teachers, patrons, schools, libraries, courts, monasteries, or universities;
- the source and receiving languages;
- what was selected, omitted, renamed, contested, or transformed;
- later movement onward, including returns and counter-influences.

The Greek–Syriac–Arabic–Hebrew–Latin history is therefore shown as a network of intellectual work, not as a story in which one culture merely stores material for another. Arabic and Islamic philosophy receives its own hall; Jewish philosophy receives its own hall; Latin Christian and Scholastic traditions receive their own hall. Doorways and threshold interpretation make their contacts visible without collapsing them into one “medieval” doctrine or treating the Islamic and Jewish halls as preludes to Latin Europe.

The same rule applies across Asia. Buddhist thought begins in South Asian settings but receives its own transregional wing; its Chinese and wider East Asian transformations appear in the East Asian continuity hall. “Transmission” here means translation, institutional development, commentary, adaptation, and debate—not the export of an unchanged package.

No tradition should exist only as a bridge to somebody else. A transmission figure’s primary exhibit must first explain that figure’s work in its own setting.

## Uncertainty, disputed dates, and controversial labels

Uncertainty is interpretation, not a data defect to hide.

- Use “traditional,” “approximately,” “flourished,” “attributed,” “legendary,” “pseudonymous,” or a date range when the evidence requires it.
- A broad date range must never be displayed as an implausibly long lifespan. Patañjali’s range concerns floruit and authorship uncertainty.
- Separate a historical person from a composite or attributed text where necessary. The Laozi installation is the model.
- State when a founder story belongs to a tradition’s self-understanding and when modern scholarship remains uncertain. Mahāvīra is a canonical tīrthaṅkara in an earlier lineage, not the inventor of Jain teaching.
- Explain the category itself when it is retrospective. “Legalism” becomes “fa/statecraft currents” in interpretation; “Continental Philosophy” is introduced as a later family resemblance, not a unified school.
- When membership is disputed, display both the status and its confidence. Do not resolve doubt through exhibit placement alone.

The most consequential adjudications and the alternatives rejected are recorded in [`difficult-placements.md`](./difficult-placements.md).

## Living and recently deceased thinkers

Living and recently deceased thinkers receive normal primary homes based on mature, documented contributions, but their interpretation remains visibly provisional.

- Do not present a living thinker as the culmination of a tradition.
- Use “present” only when the source record is current; death dates are factual metadata and must be updated promptly.
- Explain active debates rather than announcing a settled legacy.
- Review living-thinker tiers and wording on a regular editorial cycle and whenever the underlying Atlas record changes materially.
- Preserve disagreement and criticism; prominence is not endorsement.
- Reserve expansion space in modern and social-thought rooms so new records do not require rewriting older traditions as a march toward the present.

The current recently deceased set includes Jürgen Habermas; his dates and legacy language must be kept factual and open to continuing reception rather than treated as a settled endpoint.

## Review disagreements and governing resolutions

Specialists agreed on the context-first rule but identified two recurring tensions.

First, historical reviewers favored the setting in which a body of work formed, while thematic reviewers sometimes favored the field in which it is now most influential. The resolution is historical primary plus focused thematic secondary unless the mature contribution clearly reorganizes a modern field and the thematic home gives the more responsible account of the work. Aristotle, Patañjali, Dignāga, Dharmakīrti, Kant, and Foucault follow the historical rule; Beauvoir, Whitehead, Nagel, Parfit, Carnap, Popper, and Kuhn illustrate carefully stated contribution-first decisions.

Second, cultural specialists demanded independent intellectual settings, while visitor-experience reviewers favored visible connections. The resolution is **distinct halls inside connected wings, joined by thresholds rather than merged taxonomies**. That produces separate Islamic, Jewish, and Latin halls; separate South Asian and Buddhist wings; and separate Classical Chinese and East Asian continuity halls.

Two person-level disagreements were especially close. The continental reviewer favored Beauvoir’s phenomenological-existential formation; the feminist/social-thought reviewer favored Feminist Philosophies. The approved decision gives Beauvoir a Feminist Philosophies primary and an anchor-strength phenomenological/existential secondary so her formation remains legible without subordinating her independent philosophy. The analytic reviewer favored Anscombe’s action and intention work; the ethics reviewer favored the virtue revival, producing an Analytic primary with a substantial moral-philosophy secondary. These are documented adjudications, not claims that the alternative interpretation was unintelligible.

## Editorial and validation checklist

Before accepting a new or revised assignment, reviewers should be able to answer “yes” to all of the following:

- Does the record use an existing stable Atlas ID?
- Is there exactly one primary wing, hall, room, and tier?
- Does the primary home follow the decision order above?
- Are umbrella, school, field, role, and tier kept conceptually separate?
- Are disputed memberships and uncertain dates stated rather than concealed?
- Does every secondary appearance teach a named relation and point back to the primary?
- Does cross-cultural interpretation name agents and transformations rather than only endpoints?
- Does a living or recently deceased thinker remain open to revision?
- Does the assignment avoid inventing an absent branch merely to fill a hall?
- Can a non-specialist explain the placement after reading its rationale?

## Concise reference baseline

These sources are not exhaustive bibliographies. They support the classification distinctions most likely to be misunderstood:

- [SEP: Aristotle](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/aristotle/) — range of fields and long reception.
- [SEP: Ancient Skepticism](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/skepticism-ancient/) — Academic and Pyrrhonian lineages.
- [SEP: Epistemology in Classical Indian Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/epistemology-india/) and [SEP: Dharmakīrti](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/dharmakiirti/) — pramāṇa, Dignāga, Dharmakīrti, and the relation of logic to epistemology.
- [IEP: Yoga Sūtras of Patañjali](https://iep.utm.edu/yoga/) — Yoga as a philosophical and liberative system, not merely a modern mind topic.
- [SEP: Legalism in Chinese Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/chinese-legalism/) — limits of “Legalism” and the broader sense of *fa*.
- [SEP: Laozi](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/laozi/) — legendary biography, composite text, and layered Daoist reception.
- [SEP: Greek Sources in Arabic and Islamic Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/arabic-islamic-greek/), [SEP: Arabic and Islamic influence on the Latin West](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/arabic-islamic-influence/), and [SEP: Maimonides](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/maimonides/) — connected but non-identical philosophical worlds.
- [SEP: Mulla Sadra](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mulla-sadra/) — post-Avicennian, Safavid, and later Islamic philosophical continuity.
- [SEP: Kant](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/kant/), [SEP: Wittgenstein](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/wittgenstein/), [SEP: Beauvoir](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/beauvoir/), [SEP: Anscombe](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/anscombe/), [SEP: Camus](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/camus/), and [SEP: Foucault](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/foucault/) — the principal modern disputed placements.
